Executive regulation of personal Data protection Law
Based on the Executive Regulations of the Personal Data Protection Law (Ministerial
Decision No. 34/2024), here is a detailed breakdown of each article.
Chapter 1: Definitions and General Provisions
Article 1: Definitions for key legal terms are established to ensure consistent
application of the regulations. This article defines the "Law" as the Personal Data
Protection Law (Royal Decree 6/2022). It also identifies the "Competent Department" as
the Ministry's administrative division for data protection and defines "Permit," "Disclosure"
(enabling third-party access), and "Personal Data Breach" (unauthorized access leading to
data destruction or alteration).
Article 2: Entities must provide requested information or documentation to the
Ministry within 30 days. Controllers and processors are legally obligated to cooperate with
the Ministry's oversight by providing any requested records or data within this
specified timeframe.
Article 3: Controllers may contract with data processors while maintaining legal and
administrative accountability. While a controller can hire a processor to handle data on
their behalf, the processor remains independently liable for any violations of the Law or
these regulations. The processor acts as a deputy for the controller in third-party relations.
Article 4: Data processing requires explicit and documented consent from a person
with full legal capacity. For consent to be valid, it must be clear, given without coercion,
and documented in writing, electronically, or through another method defined by the
controller.
Chapter 2: Procedures for Obtaining a Permit
Article 5: A permit from the Ministry is mandatory before processing sensitive
categories of personal data. Controllers must apply for this permit before handling
sensitive data. The application must include the Data Protection Officer's (DPO) contact
info, the purpose of processing, data classifications, third-party processors, and the
locations where data will be stored.
Article 6: Permit applications must include the entity's privacy policy and breach
management procedures. When applying for a permit, the controller is required to attach
their internal personal data protection policy and a description of the precautionary
measures they have in place to handle potential data breaches.
Article 7: The Ministry provides a decision on permit applications within 45 days with a
right to appeal. The Ministry must justify any refusal of a permit. If a request is denied (or if
45 days pass without a response), the applicant can appeal to the Minister within 60 days.
Article 8: Permits are valid for five years and may be renewed using the same
procedures and fees. A permit is issued for a maximum of five years. Renewals follow the
same application requirements and fee schedules as the initial permit.
Article 9: Controllers must notify the Ministry of any changes to the information in
their permit within 15 days. If any of the data provided in the original permit application
changes, the controller must inform the Ministry using the official form within this two
week window.
Article 10: Permits can be canceled for law violations, fraud, or upon the controller's
request. The Ministry may cancel a permit if the controller violates the Law, fails to report
data changes, or if it is discovered that the permit was obtained through fraud or falsified
information.
Chapter 3: Processing the Personal Data of Children
Article 11: Processing a child's personal data requires the explicit, documented
consent of their legal guardian. Controllers may only collect the absolute minimum data
from a child necessary to verify their identity and obtain the required consent from their
parent or guardian.
Article 12: Processing child data must be for clear, safe purposes and limited to the
minimum data necessary. All processing of a child’s data must be direct, safe, and free
from any form of deception. The amount of data collected must be strictly limited to what
is needed for the stated purpose.
Article 13: Controllers must provide guardians with tools to access and update their
child's personal data. Specific means and tools must be established so that guardians
can easily view, update, or modify any personal data the controller holds regarding their
child.
Article 14: Disclosing or sharing a child's personal data with third parties requires
explicit guardian consent. Controllers and processors are strictly prohibited from sharing
a child's data with any third party unless they have obtained clear, documented permission
from the guardian.
Article 15: Protections for children apply equally to individuals who are legally
incapacitated or have limited capacity. In cases involving individuals who lack full legal
capacity, their legal guardians, trustees, or custodians act on their behalf, and all child
specific data protections apply.
Chapter 4: Rights of the Data Subject
Article 16: Data subjects may exercise their legal rights for free, and controllers must
respond within 45 days. Individuals can request to access, transport, or correct their data.
While the request is being processed, the subject can also ask the controller to temporarily
stop processing their data.
Article 17: Controllers can refuse unreasonable or repetitive requests provided they
issue a justified written refusal. A controller may deny a request if it is repetitive without
justification or requires "extraordinary effort." The refusal must be sent in writing to the
subject within the 45-day window.
Article 18: Subjects have the right to request data erasure if processing is no longer
necessary or is illegal. Erasure can be requested if the purpose of processing is fulfilled,
consent is withdrawn, or the processing violates the Law. Controllers may refuse if the data
is needed for legal obligations or active disputes.
Article 19: Data subjects are entitled to receive a clear and readable copy of their
processed personal data. Subjects can request their data in either electronic or paper
format. The controller must ensure this copy does not reveal the personal data of any other
individuals.
Article 20: Data subjects have the right to transfer their personal data to a new
controller. Subjects can request their data be moved to a different entity. The current
controller is required to perform this transfer if they are legally obligated to do so.
Chapter 5: Obligations of the Controller and Processor
Article 21: A clear privacy policy must be published in a visible location to inform
subjects of their rights. This policy must outline the mechanisms and procedures through
which data subjects can exercise the rights granted to them by the Law and these
regulations.
Article 22: Written consent and a free opt-out mechanism are required for all
marketing and commercial materials. Before sending any advertising or commercial
material, controllers must get written consent, explain the delivery method, and provide a
clear, cost-free way for the subject to stop receiving such materials.
Article 23: Certain controllers and processors must appoint an independent, Ministry
licensed external auditor. These entities must allow the auditor full access to their
systems, data, and records to verify compliance with personal data protection standards.
Article 24: The external auditor’s report must be submitted to the Ministry within 60
days of their appointment. A copy of the completed audit report must be sent to the
Ministry to ensure the entity is operating according to the legal framework.
Article 25: Sensitive data may not be published or shared except where required by
law or a court order. The categories of data that require a permit under Article 5 are strictly
confidential and cannot be disclosed unless there is a specific legal mandate or judicial
decision.
Article 26: Controllers must implement electronic systems to prevent unauthorized
access, leaks, or data tampering. These systems must include mechanisms for data
recovery in case of technical failures and must be regularly tested to ensure their
effectiveness.
Article 27: Processing documents must be retained securely for specific, legitimate
durations aligned with the purpose. The reason for keeping any processing documents
must be legal and specified. Protection systems must be used to ensure this
documentation is held safely.
Article 28: Controllers and processors must maintain a detailed Record of Processing
Activities (RoPA). This record must include DPO details, data classifications, authorized
access lists, processing timeframes, erasure methods, disclosure parties, cross-border
transfer logs, and breach history.
Article 29: The Record of Processing Activities must be kept updated and provided to
the Ministry upon request. The controller must ensure the RoPA is current and submit it to
the Ministry's competent department whenever it is requested for review.
Chapter 6: Personal Data Breaches
Article 30: The Ministry must be notified of data breaches within 72 hours if rights are
threatened. The notification must include a description of the breach, the affected data,
potential consequences, corrective actions, and contact points for further information.
Article 31: The Ministry assesses breach responses and may direct the controller to
notify affected subjects. After receiving a breach report, the Ministry evaluates the
controller's mitigation efforts and can provide guidance or support. They may also order the
controller to inform the affected individuals.
Article 32: Subjects must be notified of breaches within 72 hours if there is a high
risk of grave damage. This notice must reach the data subjects within three days and
include the nature of the breach, the specific data compromised, and recommendations
on how to minimize the impact.
Article 33: Controllers must document all breach incidents, their causes,
consequences, and corrective actions taken. A detailed log of every breach event and
the technical or organizational steps taken to address it must be maintained for the period
specified by the Ministry.
Chapter 7: Data Protection Officer (DPO)
Article 34: The Data Protection Officer (DPO) must be professionally qualified and
knowledgeable in privacy laws. The DPO is required to have the competence to handle
data protection issues and a deep understanding of the Law, these regulations, and the
entity's internal privacy practices.
Article 35: The DPO advises the organization, monitors legal compliance, and
coordinates with the Ministry. The DPO's role is to provide consultations, oversee the
implementation of privacy policies, and act as the primary point of contact for the
Ministry regarding data processing.
Article 36: DPO contact information must be published to allow subjects to
communicate regarding their data. The controller must make the DPO’s name and
contact details available so that individuals can discuss any matters related to the
processing of their personal data.
Chapter 8: Transferring Personal Data Outside the Borders
Article 37: Cross-border transfers require explicit consent and must not compromise
national security interests. Transferring data outside Oman is prohibited if it harms state
interests. Consent is not required for data sent under international treaties Oman has
joined or for fully anonymized data.
Article 38: Controllers must ensure that the external processing party provides an
equivalent level of data protection. Before any transfer, the controller must guarantee
that the destination country or entity offers a level of protection at least equal to Omani
Law and these regulations.
Article 39: A risk assessment must be conducted for cross-border transfers covering
data sensitivity and duration. The assessment must detail the data's nature, volume,
purpose, shared parties, and whether the transfer is a one-time event or a regular, recurring
process.
Article 40: The Ministry has the right to request a copy of the cross-border transfer risk
assessment report. The Ministry may review these reports at any time to verify that the
external processing parties provide sufficient protection for Omani data.
Chapter 9: Complaints and Penalties
Article 41: Subjects can file complaints with the Ministry regarding violations within 30
days of discovery. Individuals or interested parties can report breaches of the Law or
regulations. The Ministry will then notify the controller of the complaint within seven days.
Article 42: Controllers have 14 days to respond to a complaint once they have been
notified by the Ministry. The controller is given this two-week period to provide their
official response or defense regarding the reported violation.
Article 43: The Ministry must decide on complaints within 60 days; silence is deemed a
rejection. The Ministry will review the complaint and the controller's response to issue a
decision. If no response is provided within 60 days, the complaint is considered rejected.
Article 44: Violations can result in warnings, permit suspension, permit cancellation,
or fines up to OMR 2,000. The Minister has the authority to impose these administrative
penalties depending on the severity of the violation.
Article 45: Administrative penalties can be appealed to the Minister within 60 days of
the notification. An entity that has been penalized can file a grievance. The Minister must
provide a decision within 30 days, or the appeal is considered rejected.

Personal Data Protection Policy for Units of the State Administrative Apparatus Document
I. Core Definitions (Sections 3 & 4)
Key terms are defined to distinguish roles between government controllers and third
party processors. The "Control Unit" is the government entity that determines the purpose
and means of processing, while the "Processing Unit" or "Third Party" handles data on the
Control Unit's behalf. Personal Data is defined as any information identifying a natural
person, such as a Civil Number, name, or genetic identity.
Technical terms define the lifecycle of data from initial processing to destruction and
cross-border movement. "Processing" includes any operation like collection, analysis, or
storage. A "Data Breach" is unauthorized access leading to data destruction or alteration,
while "Erasure" involves making data unrecoverable. "Transfer" specifically refers to
moving data across Oman’s geographical borders.
II. Obligations of the Control Unit (Article 1)
Article 1.1: Control Units are obligated to protect all personal data in their possession,
including data received from other units. Government entities must safeguard all
information they hold, regardless of whether they collected it directly or received it from
another state administrative unit.
Article 1.2: Processing must adhere to the principles of lawfulness, fairness, accuracy,
and storage limitation. Data must be collected through honest and legal means,
kept accurate and updated, and should not be stored in a way that identifies the subject
once the original purpose of processing is fulfilled.
Article 1.3: Units must practice data minimization by collecting only the absolute
minimum information required for a service. When requesting documents or data from a
citizen, the government unit must limit the request to what is strictly necessary to
complete the transaction, especially if the data is not available electronically from other
government sources.
Article 1.4: Requesting a government service implies the subject's knowledge and
consent for necessary data processing. The policy assumes that when an individual
applies for a service, they have consented to the processing and disclosure of their data to
other relevant government units to fulfill their request.
Article 1.5 & 1.6: Robust technical and organizational measures must be implemented
to prevent unauthorized access or accidental loss. Units must establish security
safeguards for all systems and storage media to prevent hacking, unauthorized disclosure,
or accidental destruction of personal information.
Article 1.7 & 1.8: Engaging third-party processors requires a written contract,
verification of safeguards, and audit rights. When a unit uses an external processor,
there must be a written contract specifying data retention periods and deletion
requirements. The Control Unit also maintains the right to audit the security measures
used by the third party.
Article 1.9: Units must publish a clear Privacy Notice on their official websites to
inform the public of data practices. This notice must explain the purpose of data
collection, the types of data processed, the identity of the controller, and the rights of the
data subject.
Article 1.10: Mechanisms must be in place for the secure and permanent destruction
of data to prevent unauthorized recovery. Once the purpose of processing is finished, the
unit must ensure the data is destroyed in a way that makes it impossible for unauthorized
parties to retrieve or view it again.
Article 1.11: The National Center for Electronic Defense must be
notified immediately in the event of any data breach or leak. Control Units have a
mandatory obligation to report any unauthorized access, leak, or destruction of personal
data to the center as soon as it is discovered.
Article 1.12 & 1.13: Data sovereignty requires processing within Oman, with overseas
transfers requiring specialized approval. Processing must generally stay within Oman's
borders to ensure national sovereignty. Any transfer outside the country requires prior
approval from the National Center for Electronic Defense, unless it is for contract
execution, legal defense, or protecting the subject's vital interests.
III. Processor Obligations and Policy Governance
Section 2: Processing Units must act strictly according to the Control Unit's
instructions and maintain high security standards. Third parties or processors must
protect the data they handle and are prohibited from taking any action on the data unless
specifically instructed by the government Control Unit.
Implementation and Monitoring: MTCIT oversees the policy, with a 24-month window
for units to reach full compliance. The Ministry of Transport, Communications and
Information Technology (MTCIT) owns the policy and monitors its implementation across
the state apparatus. Results of compliance monitoring are reported directly to the Council
of Ministers.
IV. Rights of the Data Subject (Annex)
Subjects have the right to be informed of the purpose and necessity of data
collection. The Privacy Notice must inform individuals whether their data is being
collected for a mandatory or optional purpose and notify them if their data will be
processed outside of Oman.
Individuals are granted the right to access, correct, and update their personal data
held by government units. Data subjects have the right to obtain a clear, readable copy of
their information and request that any inaccurate or incomplete data be corrected or
updated.
Website and Data Hosting Policy
Based on the Website and Data Hosting Policy, here is the detailed breakdown of the
sections and policy statements as requested.
I. Foundational Framework (Sections 1 – 2)
Section 1 (Introduction): Government agencies must maintain high-quality and secure
online access for public e-services. As the internet becomes the primary channel for
public interaction, this policy provides the necessary direction for agencies to ensure that
their websites and web applications—whether managed internally or outsourced—remain
reliable and secure.
Section 2 (Purpose): The policy aims to secure the government's web presence and
coordinate the adoption of cloud services. The primary objectives are to build public
trust through secure web platforms for citizens and businesses, while facilitating a
managed approach to the adoption of cloud computing across the government.
Section 2.1 (Scope of Applicability): This policy applies to all government websites,
web applications, and entities managing sensitive state data. The scope covers internal
and third-party developers, mobile applications, and any public or private entity engaged in
the management or hosting of sensitive government information.
Section 2.2 (Definitions): Technical terms such as Cloud Computing and standard
service models (SaaS, PaaS, IaaS) are defined. This section establishes clear definitions
for Websites, Web Portals, and Mobile Apps. It specifically adopts the NIST models for
cloud computing, distinguishing between software, platform, and infrastructure services to
ensure technical clarity across agencies.
II. Policy Statements (Section 3)
Section 3.1 (Government Websites): Agencies must implement high availability,
security assessments, and bilingual content standards. Websites must follow W3C
recommendations for a "One-Web-Approach" to ensure accessibility across all devices,
including for the disabled. Security requirements include mandatory annual independent
assessments, the use of the .om domain, and the publication of a clear privacy policy and
terms of use.
Section 3.2 (Prepare for Data Security): Government data must be classified according
to Royal Decree 118/2011 and risk-assessed. Agencies and private partners must handle
data with "due care," ensuring that all information systems are classified and that risk
assessments are shared with senior management for their consent on treatment
approaches.
Section 3.3 (Data Hosting and Cloud Services): Government data should be hosted
within Oman's borders and must be encrypted on the cloud. Cloud adoption
is permitted only after security risks are mitigated. As a "rule of thumb," data must remain
within the geographical boundaries of Oman; any exceptions require approval from
the Cabinet of Ministers. Furthermore, agencies must use contractual agreements to
prohibit the use of unauthorized external repositories like Dropbox or Google Drive.
III. Governance and Compliance (Sections 4 – 5)
Section 4.1 (Policy Management): The Information Technology Authority (ITA) is
responsible for creating and maintaining this policy. The ITA holds the overall
responsibility for facilitating policy implementation and providing guidance and advice to
all government agencies regarding their hosting and web standards.
Section 4.2 (Policy Implementation): Agencies are responsible for compliance, while
the ITA conducts periodic security assessments. All government owners and
administrators must implement and report on these standards. The ITA will verify this
through periodical policy compliance checks and security assessments of published web
applications.
Section 5 (Related References): The policy is supported by a framework of laws
including the eTransaction Law and Cyber Security Guidelines. Implementation of these
standards should be viewed in conjunction with the Data Classification Scheme (RD
118/2011), Risk Management Frameworks, and upcoming data privacy legislation.
AI Policy Doc
Based on the Public Policy for Safe and Ethical Use of Artificial Intelligence Systems
(April 2025), here is the detailed breakdown of its sections and guiding principles.
I. Foundational Framework and Scope
Introduction: The policy establishes a framework for the safe and ethical use of AI to
promote sustainable development and human rights in Oman. This policy aims to
ensure that AI systems are human-centric, respecting basic rights such as privacy,
equality, and justice in consistency with Islamic principles. It serves as a strategic guide to
enhance trust in AI technologies while supporting the goals of Oman Vision 2040.
Terms and Definitions: Technical and ethical terms are defined to provide a clear legal
and operational scope for AI governance. The policy defines Artificial Intelligence
(AI) as a field focused on simulating human cognitive abilities like vision, problem-solving,
and decision-making. It specifically addresses Generative AI (GenAI), which uses neural
networks to create new content based on training data. Other defined terms
include Fairness (avoiding bias), Transparency (understanding decision-making
processes), and Explainability (providing justifications for outcomes).
Policy Objectives: The policy aims to govern the development and use of AI through a
combination of technical controls and ethical principles. The objective is to establish a
comprehensive framework that ensures responsible and secure use, thereby fostering
innovation and increasing Oman's competitiveness on both regional and international
levels.
Scope of Implementation: This policy applies to all government units and private
sector organizations involved in the AI lifecycle. The scope covers every stage of
implementation, including data collection, system design, training, operation, and
continuous evaluation of AI systems.
II. Policy Statements and Controls
General Statements: Entities must fully comply with technical controls and ethical
principles, including for Generative AI systems. Subject entities are required to
periodically evaluate system performance to ensure data integrity, output accuracy, and
the rectification of any biases or harmful content. Regulatory authorities are expected to
align their specific frameworks with this national policy.
Requirements for the Use of AI Systems: Entities utilizing AI must ensure human
supervision and the ability to explain system decisions. Entities must provide
mechanisms for human control over sensitive and impactful decisions. Systems must be
objectively evaluated for viability and added value before adoption, and their performance
must be continuously monitored for errors or negative impacts. Transparency in decision
making mechanisms and established accountability frameworks are mandatory.
Data Management for AI Users: Personal data use in AI must be restricted to
authorized purposes and protected by local laws. Data used in AI systems must be
classified to ensure appropriate protection, utilizing advanced measures like encryption
and multi-factor authentication. Entities must also implement clear policies for data
retention and ensure secure deletion once the data is no longer needed.
Requirements for Developing AI Systems: Developers must document the
development process and ensure solutions are sustainable and ethical. The
development process, including the system's purpose and the data used, must be clearly
documented for future reference. Developers are tasked with creating technologically
sustainable solutions and conducting ethical, social, and environmental impact
assessments prior to deployment.
Transparency in Development: Measures must be implemented to prevent system
misuse and ensure the clarity of AI-generated content. Developers must provide
mechanisms to identify generated content through labeling or explanatory notices to
prevent the spread of harmful or misleading information. They must also provide
documentation that explains decision-making logic and data analysis processes.
Developer Accountability and Security: Developers must monitor for biases and
grant individuals rights over their data within the system. Continuous monitoring for
data or algorithm-related biases is required to ensure fairness and inclusiveness.
Furthermore, developers must grant individuals the right to access their data, correct
errors, or request data deletion through user-friendly interfaces.
III. Ethical Principles and Compliance
Humanity and Society: AI must respect human dignity and promote societal well
being and environmental protection. AI technologies should be harnessed for sectors like
healthcare and education while supporting sustainable development by improving energy
efficiency. The policy emphasizes the right to human intervention in sensitive decision
making processes.
Inclusivity and Justice: Systems must be designed to prevent bias and ensure
affordable access for all segments of society. Algorithms must be audited to ensure no
bias occurs based on race, gender, or religion, in alignment with Islamic principles. Training
data should be diverse enough to represent minorities and individuals with disabilities, and
the government will collaborate with the private sector to keep these technologies
affordable.
Responsibility and Accountability: Clear mechanisms for breach management,
accountability, and system auditing must be established. Systems must be secure from
breaches and avoid causing physical or psychological harm. Entities must provide clear
complaint channels and rectification mechanisms for damages, and ensure systems are
"audit-able" through performance logs and independent technical audits.
Policy Compliance and Management: The Ministry and regulatory authorities are
responsible for monitoring and reporting compliance. The Ministry of Transport,
Communications and Information Technology monitors government units and reports
findings to the Council of Ministers. Regulatory authorities oversee compliance within their
specific sectors. This policy is owned by the Ministry and will be revised whenever
necessary.
The Cloud Computing Policy
Based on the Cloud Computing Policy issued by the Ministry of Transport,
Communications and Information Technology (MTCIT) in May 2021, here is a detailed
breakdown of its sections and objectives.
I. Foundational Framework
Introduction: The policy provides the necessary guidance for government units to
adopt cloud computing safely and effectively. This section establishes the context for
the Sultanate's digital transformation, framing cloud computing as a key enabler for
modernizing government services and improving operational efficiency.
Policy Goals: The objectives are centered on optimizing IT spending, enhancing
service scalability, and ensuring data security. The policy aims to provide a unified
framework for cloud adoption that reduces the cost of infrastructure for individual agencies
while maintaining high standards of data protection and availability.
Policy Scope: These regulations apply to all units of the State Administrative
Apparatus and entities handling government data. The scope ensures that any public
sector organization or third party managing government information systems on the cloud
must adhere to these specific technical and administrative standards.
Overview of Cloud Computing Services: This section defines the various service
models (SaaS, PaaS, IaaS) and deployment types. It provides the technical definitions
necessary for agencies to understand the different levels of cloud engagement
and determines which models are appropriate for various types of government data and
applications.
Advantages of Cloud Computing: The policy highlights the benefits of agility, cost
reduction, and enhanced disaster recovery. By migrating to the cloud, government units
can benefit from "pay-as-you-go" financial models, rapid deployment of new services, and
superior data backup capabilities compared to traditional on-premise hosting.
II. Policy Clauses and Requirements
Data Classification: All data must be categorized before migration
to determine the appropriate cloud environment. In alignment with national data
classification standards, agencies must assess whether data is "Open," "Restricted,"
"Confidential," or "Secret" to ensure it is hosted on a cloud platform with the required
security level.
Security Requirements: Entities must implement robust technical controls, including
encryption and identity management. This clause mandates the use of advanced
security measures to protect data at rest and in transit, ensuring that cloud environments
meet the Sultanate's national cybersecurity standards.
Administrative Requirements: Agencies must establish clear governance, service
level agreements (SLAs), and incident response plans. The policy requires formal
administrative oversight of cloud contracts, ensuring that government
units maintain control over their data and have clear protocols for managing service
interruptions or provider transitions.
Hosting Outside the Sultanate: Strictly controlled conditions are set for storing
government data or applications on foreign cloud platforms. As a general rule,
government data should be hosted within Oman; however, this section outlines the
exceptional cases and the specific approvals required (often involving the Cabinet of
Ministers) for overseas hosting.
III. Governance and Compliance
Ownership and Review: The MTCIT is the official owner of this policy and is
responsible for its periodic update. The Ministry manages the lifecycle of the policy,
ensuring it remains relevant to technological advancements and evolving national security
requirements.
Policy Compliance: All addressed entities are legally obligated to adhere to the
standards set forth in this document. The policy establishes the enforcement framework,
requiring agencies to demonstrate compliance during audits and ensuring that cloud
adoption does not bypass national security or data protection laws.

General Policy for the Safe and Ethical Use of Artificial Intelligence Systems (April 2025)
Based on the General Policy for the Safe and Ethical Use of Artificial Intelligence
Systems (April 2025), here is the detailed breakdown of each section and principle.
I. Foundational Framework and Definitions
Introduction: The policy establishes a strategic framework to ensure AI development
aligns with human rights, Islamic principles, and Oman Vision 2040. This section
positions AI as a pivotal tool for improving efficiency and sustainable development. It
emphasizes that the rapid expansion of AI necessitates a framework grounded in security,
privacy, and trust to protect human dignity and ensure justice.
Terms and Definitions: Core technical and ethical terms are defined to create a unified
understanding for all government and private entities.
• Artificial Intelligence (AI): Systems simulating human cognitive abilities like
problem-solving and decision-making.
• Generative AI (GenAI): A branch of AI that uses neural networks to create new
content based on trained data patterns.
• AI Systems: Software or hardware designed for specific tasks with varying levels of
autonomy.
• Using/Developing Entities: Any government or private institution that operates or
builds AI systems to achieve specific goals.
• Explainability: The ability of an AI system to provide clear justifications and reasons
for its decisions and outcomes.
II. Policy Objectives and Scope
Policy Objectives: The framework aims to govern AI through technical and ethical
controls that enhance trust and national competitiveness. The policy seeks to provide a
comprehensive guide that balances innovation with responsible use, ensuring that AI
systems are secure, fair, and beneficial to Omani society.
Scope of Implementation: This policy applies to the entire AI lifecycle across all
government units and private sector organizations. The regulations cover every stage,
including data collection, system design, training, operation, and continuous evaluation,
ensuring a consistent standard of safety and ethics.
III. Policy Clauses and Requirements
General Clauses: All entities must comply with technical and ethical standards, and
the Ministry will oversee the legislative environment. Entities are required to evaluate
system performance periodically to ensure data integrity and output accuracy. Regulatory
bodies are tasked with aligning their sectoral policies with this national framework and may
add specific regulations with Ministry approval.
Controls for Using AI Systems: Entities using AI must maintain human oversight and
provide clear explanations for automated decisions.
• Human Supervision: Mandatory for sensitive or impactful decisions to ensure
human control over AI outcomes.
• Explainability: Systems must provide understandable justifications, particularly in
health, employment, and financial services.
• Data Protection: Personal data use must be restricted to authorized purposes,
classified, and protected via encryption and multi-factor authentication.
• Accountability: Decisions must be documented for future verification and
auditing.
Controls for Developing AI Systems: Developers are required to document their
processes and ensure systems are sustainable and ethical.
• Sustainability: Solutions must be technically and environmentally sustainable for
long-term use.
• Impact Assessments: Ethical, social, and environmental assessments must be
conducted before a system is published.
• Transparency: Generative AI must have mechanisms to label or identify AI
generated content to prevent misinformation.
• Individual Rights: Developers must provide tools for individuals to access, correct,
or delete their personal data within the system.
• Cybersecurity: Best practices, such as regular software updates and threat
management, must be followed.
IV. Ethical Principles and Governance
Humanity and Society: AI must promote societal well-being and respect human
dignity during all sensitive decision-making. This principle ensures AI is harnessed for
sectors like health and education while protecting the environment and ensuring the fair
distribution of technological benefits across society.
Inclusivity and Justice: Systems must be audited for bias to ensure fair access for all
segments of society, including minorities. Training data must be diverse to prevent
discrimination based on race, gender, or religion, in alignment with Islamic values. The
government also aims to keep these technologies affordable through public-private
partnerships.
Responsibility and Accountability: Entities must provide complaint channels and
ensure systems are secure and auditable. This section requires that AI systems do not
cause physical or psychological harm. It mandates the use of technical logs and
performance reviews so that systems can be independently audited by regulatory bodies.
Policy Compliance and Management: The Ministry monitors government compliance,
while regulatory bodies oversee their respective sectors. The Ministry of Transport,
Communications and Information Technology is the owner of this policy and reports
compliance results to the Council of Ministers. The policy is subject to review as needed
to keep pace with global technological changes.
Related Frameworks: This policy operates alongside existing laws, including the PDPL
and Oman's Cybercrime Law. Implementation must be viewed in conjunction with
national programs for AI, data classification schemes (RD 118/2011), and information risk
management frameworks.
Sultanate of Oman Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL)
Based on the summary of the Sultanate of Oman Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL),
here is the detailed breakdown of its sections, definitions, and requirements.
General Framework and Enforcement
The Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL) is the primary regulation for data processing
in Oman.
The Law came into force on February 13, 2023, while the Executive Regulations followed
on February 5, 2024, providing a mandatory compliance deadline for all entities by
February 5, 2025,.
The Ministry of Transport, Communications & Information Technology serves as the
competent authority for enforcement.
The Ministry oversees compliance and can impose administrative fines up to OMR 2,000,
while courts have the authority to impose fines ranging from OMR 500 up to OMR 500,000
for breaches of the Law.
Oman’s Law features unique elements such as a single legal basis for processing and
extensive exclusions.
Unlike similar regional laws, the PDPL relies solely on explicit consent as a legal basis for
processing; it also provides a substantial list of cases where the Law does not apply at all,
such as when processing is required for the performance of a legal obligation or a contract.
Definitions and Scope
Personal data is broadly defined as any information that makes a natural person
identifiable.
This includes identifiers like names, ID numbers, online identifiers, and location data, as
well as factors related to physical, genetic, or social identity. If an individual cannot
be identified from the data (e.g., anonymized data), the Law does not apply.
Processing certain sensitive data categories requires a special permit from the
Ministry.
While "sensitive data" is not explicitly defined, Article 5 requires a permit to process
categories such as genetic and vital data (e.g., blood pressure), health data, ethnic origin,
religious beliefs, criminal records, and sexual life,.
The Law regulates the processing of personal data across all types, sources, and
forms.
The scope covers any form of data—electronic or paper, structured or unstructured—
whether provided directly by individuals or obtained by an entity from other sources,.
Roles and Obligations
Entities are classified as either Controllers or Processors depending on their role in
data management.
A Controller determines the purpose and means of processing, while
a Processor handles personal data strictly on behalf of the Controller,.
Controllers have extensive obligations, including obtaining explicit consent
and establishing technical controls.
They must implement risk assessments, cross-border transfer procedures, and technical
measures to ensure compliance while also providing written privacy notices to data
subjects before processing begins,.
Controllers must cooperate with Ministry oversight, including audits and mandatory
breach notifications.
Responsibilities include appointing external auditors if requested, maintaining document
management timelines, and notifying both the Ministry and data subjects immediately in
the event of a personal data breach.
Controllers are responsible for appointing a Data Protection Officer and ensuring data
confidentiality.
They must appoint a DPO according to regulatory requirements, ensure the confidentiality
of data, and obtain specific written consent before publishing data or sending commercial
marketing materials.
Processors are similarly obligated to comply with Ministry controls and cooperate
with investigations.
Processors must follow all procedures established by the Ministry, appoint external
auditors upon request, and manage processing documents according to the timelines
specified in the Executive Regulations,.
Principles and Lawful Basis
Seven core principles guide the lawful and ethical processing of personal data in
Oman.
These principles are: Lawfulness, Fairness, and Transparency; Purpose Limitation; Data
Minimization; Storage Limitation; Accuracy; Integrity and Confidentiality;
and Accountability,,.
While explicit consent is the primary basis for processing, Article 3 provides several
key exceptions.
The Law does not apply to processing for national security, public interest, the
performance of state administrative powers, legal obligations, or the protection of vital and
economic interests of the country,.
Additional exceptions allow processing for contract performance, personal contexts,
and authorized research.
Exemptions also cover data available to the public and processing for statistical, scientific,
or historical research, provided the data is not attributed to an identified natural person,.
Rights of the Data Subject
Data subjects are granted comprehensive rights to control and access their personal
information.
Individuals have the right to be informed about the purpose of processing, the right to
access their data, and the right to withdraw their consent at any time,.
Individuals can request that their data be corrected, blocked, or erased.
Subjects may request the correction of inaccurate data, the blocking of processing, or the
total erasure of their data unless it is required for national record-keeping,,.
The Law ensures the right to data portability and notification in the event of a breach.
Data subjects have the right to obtain a readable copy of their data, request its transfer to
another controller, and must be notified of any violations or
breaches regarding their information,.
Sultanate of Oman Personal Data Protection Law Series: Part 2 – Summary of the Executive
Regulations
Based on the Sultanate of Oman Personal Data Protection Law Series: Part 2 –
Summary of the Executive Regulations, here is a detailed breakdown of the articles and
key requirements.
Governance and Coordination
Article 34: The Data Protection Officer (DPO) must be a professionally competent
individual familiar with Omani privacy practices. The DPO must be qualified to handle all
issues related to data protection correctly and regularly. They are required to be familiar
with both the Law and its Executive Regulations to ensure the
organization remains compliant.
Article 35: The DPO is responsible for providing internal consultations and
coordinating with the Ministry. Key duties include proposing compliance strategies to
their organization, monitoring the implementation of internal privacy policies, and serving
as the primary point of contact for the Ministry on all data processing matters.
Article 36: Controllers must publish the DPO’s contact details to allow for direct
communication with data subjects. The name and contact information of the DPO
should be included in privacy notices. This ensures that individuals can easily contact the
DPO regarding any matters related to the processing of their personal information.
Ministry Oversight and General Obligations
Article 2: Entities are obligated to provide any documents or information to the
Ministry within 30 days. Whether acting as a controller or a processor, organizations must
cooperate with the Ministry by supplying additional data or documentation within this
specified timeframe upon request.
Article 21: A personal data protection policy must be published in a visible location for
subjects to inspect. This policy—which functions as a privacy notice—must be accessible
before any data is processed. It is required to include at least the mechanisms and
procedures for individuals to exercise their legal rights.
Article 4: Explicit consent for data processing must be clear, uncoerced, and
documented. Consent must be obtained from a legally competent person before
processing begins. While the controller determines the specific means, the approval must
be in a clear format, such as writing or an electronic form.
Data Subject Rights
Article 16 & 17: Subjects may exercise their rights for free, with the
controller required to decide within 45 days. Individuals can submit written requests to
exercise their rights under the Law at no cost. Controllers may refuse a request only in
specific cases, such as if the request is "unjustifiably repetitive" or requires "extraordinary
effort".
Article 18: Data subjects have the right to request the erasure of their data under
specific conditions. Erasure can be requested if the processing purpose is finished,
consent is revoked, or the processing violates the Law. Rejection of this request
is permitted if the data is needed for a legal obligation, a court ruling, or an ongoing
dispute.
Article 19: Individuals are entitled to a clear, readable copy of their processed
personal data. Subjects can request their data in electronic or paper format. The
controller must ensure that this copy does not contain any identifying information
belonging to other persons.
Article 20: The right to data portability allows subjects to move their data to a new
controller. If a legal obligation exists, the current controller is mandated to transfer the
subject's personal data to the new designated controller.
Communication and Lifecycle Management
Article 22: Written consent is mandatory before sending any marketing or commercial
communications. Controllers must notify the subject of the channels being used
and provide a free mechanism to opt out. Any request to stop receiving communications
must be honored immediately.
Article 28: Controllers and processors must maintain a comprehensive Record of
Processing Activities (RoPA). The RoPA must include DPO details, data categories,
authorized access lists, processing purposes, retention periods, disclosure entities, cross
border transfer logs, and breach history.
Article 29: The RoPA must be updated continuously and submitted to the Ministry
upon request. The organization is responsible for ensuring the processing record is always
current and available for the Ministry's competent department to review.
Breach Management and Sensitive Data
Article 30: The Ministry must be notified of data breaches within 72 hours if rights are
at risk. This notification must detail the nature of the breach, its potential effects, and the
technical or organizational corrective measures the controller is taking to address the
situation.
Article 32: Data subjects must be notified within 72 hours if a breach is likely to
cause significant damage. Notification to individuals is mandatory in high-risk cases. It
must include the type of breach, the specific data impacted, and recommendations for the
subject to mitigate the impact.
Articles 5 – 8: Processing sensitive personal data requires a multi-step permit process
from the Ministry. The controller must apply to the Ministry with their privacy policy and
breach response plans attached. The Minister issues these permits for a maximum of five
years, and renewals follow the same procedure.
Cross-Border Data Transfers
Article 37: Overseas transfers require express consent and must protect national
security. Consent is not required if the transfer is part of an international agreement Oman
has joined or if the data is fully anonymized.
Article 38: External recipients must provide a level of protection at least equal to
Omani law. The controller is responsible for ensuring that the party receiving the data
outside Oman can provide adequate security and legal safeguards.
Article 39 & 40: A detailed risk assessment must be conducted for all cross-border
data movements. This assessment must cover the sensitivity of the data, the purpose and
stages of the transfer, and the potential impact on data subjects. The Ministry may request
a copy of this assessment report at any time.
